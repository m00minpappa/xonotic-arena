01

The Engine

Xonotic runs sixteen game modes on one set of physics — movement, weapons, and item timing never change, only the win condition does. The card game copies that exactly: master these five systems once, and every mode is a four-line rules module on top of them.

Movement is king

Momentum

Moving builds it; standing still bleeds it. High Momentum makes you airborne — a hard target with an extra action — but you can't grab items while flying. Speed vs. control, every turn.

Setup & payoff

Board Tokens

Weapons leave state behind — blobs, in-flight rockets, armed mortars, mines. A later card cashes them in. "Play card, deal damage" becomes real combos.

Risk / reward

The Stack

Health + armor to 200. Above 100 it rots; below 100 health regenerates. Overstacking off a Mega is a decaying asset — fight now, don't bank it.

Loadout identity

Ammo Economy

Four ammo types. Your weapons decide your economy and your deck's whole character. The Blaster costs nothing — the one tool every deck always has.

Map control

Item Timing

Megas and powerups sit on the Arena Row on respawn dials. Controlling when they come back up is a sub-game that decides duels on its own.

Same core, new goal

Mode Modules

Combat is identical in every mode. A single Mode Card swaps the win condition, the team structure, and what spawns on the map. That's the whole design.

// One turn — fast and lethal

1

Upkeep

Tick item dials. Resolve Stack rot/regen. Detonate in-flight tokens — mortars arm, blobs go live, rockets can trigger.

2

Move

Travel the row, build Momentum, grab any item you're standing on (only if grounded).

3

Fire

Spend Momentum + ammo to play weapons. Combos resolve in the order you choose.

4

React

Opponent may play Reaction cards during your Fire — Dodge, Air Control, Airshot punishes.

02

Multiplayer & FFA

The two-player Arena Row is just the special case where the map is unrolled into a line. For 3–6 players it closes into a Ring — and every proximity, splash, and item rule falls out of that one change. Range becomes the shortest number of steps around the loop.

Same range bands as the line: 0 steps = Point-Blank, 1 = Close, 2 = Mid, 3+ = Long — measured the short way around. Spawn points sit opposite each other; items sit on the flanks and center. Use a 6-node ring for 3–4 players, an 8-node ring for 5–6.

Speed sets tempo

Initiative

A round = every living Fighter takes one turn. Order is by highest Momentum first — the fast player dictates the fight — ties broken by lowest stack (the underdog moves first). Reactions still let a slow player interrupt.

Zone decks scale up

Splash & Targeting

Single-target weapons pick one enemy in range. Splash weapons hit every Fighter at the target location — so Mortar, Electro, Hagar and Devastator get far stronger the more players crowd a lane. Knockback can shove foes into each other's fire.

Last hit wins

Frag Credit

The Fighter who lands the killing blow scores the frag. Kill-stealing is real and intended — chipping a target to 5 and watching someone else finish them is part of the FFA texture. No assists.

No snowball

Spawn Shield

A just-respawned Fighter returns at an open spawn with base stack, half incoming damage for one turn, and can't be the target of more than one attacker until they act. Kills spawn-camping before it starts.

Leader control

The Bounty

Optional FFA rule: the current frag leader is worth +1 frag to whoever downs them and their item pickups are halved. Keeps a runaway leader honest without a hard rubber-band.

Squads

Teams

For team modes, group Fighters by color. Friendly fire off (splash still knocks allies around, no damage); the Mode Card's win condition is shared. Turn order still runs by Momentum, interleaving the teams.

03

Avatars

Your Fighter isn't a token — it's an Avatar card you place on the board. It marks your location and proximity, and it carries an always-on buff + debuff plus a limited-use Signature. You pick who you are (the avatar) separately from what you carry (the deck) — two identity layers that combo. This roster is modeled on one real playgroup: buffs, nerfs and reputations all intact.

◈

Draft first, reveal decks second. Players lock in avatars before decks are shown, so the pick can read and counter the table — a room full of snipers makes slipperysalamander's cloak look very good, and a stack of splash decks is what booger's Rally is for. Buffs and nerfs are passive; the Signature is a once-per-round or once-per-game trump card. Affinity tells you the archetype an avatar wants, but any of them can pilot any deck.

04

The Arsenal

Sixteen weapon cards, each with a Primary and Secondary fire — pick the mode as you play it. The left edge and ammo chip are color-coded by ammo type: your weapon mix is your economy.

05

Movement & Tech

The cards that make speed a system instead of flavor. Most are free or cost Momentum; Reactions are held and played on the opponent's turn. A pure-movement deck can win Race with barely a shot fired.

06

Items & Powerups

Supplied by the map (on respawn dials) or slotted into constructed decks. Powerups run for a fixed number of turns and reward the aggression the timing window demands.

07

Game Modes

Same engine, one swapped card. Each mode changes only the win condition, team structure, and what spawns — never how combat works. Race and Nexball are the proof it holds: modes where you barely shoot still play, because movement is a real system.

08

Starter Decks

Three tuned archetypes to learn on. Ammo forces commitment — you can't run everything, so each deck wants a different range and a different rhythm.

◈

Deckbuilding rule of thumb: ~40 cards — about 10 weapons sharing 1–2 ammo types, ~12 movement/tech, ~8 reactions, and ~6 powerups plus a few flex slots. Limits: max 3 of any card, max 2 of any single powerup, 6 powerups total. The core weapons (Blaster, Shotgun, Machine Gun) are commons every deck may run. The rulebook builds a full 40-card signature deck for each of the ten avatars.
